About the Project

Land records serve as the invisible backbone of modern governance. From the high-precision requirements of tax collection to the long-range vision needed for urban planning, the accuracy of these records dictates a state's efficiency. However, a persistent "spatial gap" has historically separated the mathematical exactness required by surveyors from the analytical flexibility needed by GIS professionals.

While early friction stemmed from CAD files and physical paper maps, the modern challenge is more nuanced. The current transformation isn't just a leap from paper to digital; it is an evolution from earlier-generation GIS formats and isolated applications toward a truly integrated ecosystem.

Over the past few years, Israel has launched a major national initiative to modernize its cadastral infrastructure. This effort goes far beyond a simple software upgrade, it represents a broad rethinking of how land administration is managed and maintained. By gradually addressing the accumulated limitations of older GIS systems, which have become less flexible over time and unsupported, the state is laying the groundwork for the demands of 2026 and beyond.

The initiative focuses on four critical pillars of modernization:

  • Redesigned Data Structures: Moving away from outdated formats to a streamlined, modern cadastral schema.
  • The ETL Challenge: A massive effort to Extract, Transform, and Load (ETL) decades of existing data, standardizing it to ensure consistency across the board.
  • Workflow Reimagination: Replacing siloed, manual update processes with transparent, valid, and efficient workflows that align with global best practices.
  • Integrated Decision-Making: Rebuilding the platforms and applications that consume this data, ensuring that the legal record and the spatial map are no longer inconsistent.

Although many earlier attempts were made to achieve these, none fully succeeded due to previous technological or organizational limitations. Here, by embracing advanced technologies such as Esri’s Parcel Fabric, the project finally positioned to deliver a sustainable, innovative, and future-ready cadastral infrastructure for the state.

Finally, though the project supports many institutions and organizations, it places the public at its center.

Goals & Vision

  • Applying faster modifications in the cadastral data by automating the edits workflow (as much as possible), saving time and effort for GIS editors .
  • Flexible system that can adapt to changes in technology or organization needs in the near or far future.
  • Keep the data accurate and valid.
  • Implement new logic of 3D cadaster.
  • Keep up to date with newer technology.
  • Gain an option to migrate to cloud storage in future.

Timeline & Historical Context

The Survey of Israel traces its roots back to 1920, when it was established as the Survey Department under the British Mandate. Following the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948, it became the Survey of Israel and was later placed under the Ministry of Construction and Housing.

The country’s first national digital cadastral database emerged in the mid-1990s. Much of it was built by digitizing historical paper maps—some originally produced by Ottoman-era cartographers and later refined by British surveyors. While groundbreaking at the time, this process also carried forward decades of legacy assumptions and constraints.

Between the late 1990s and 2020, a growing number of applications were developed around this core database to support regulatory changes and evolving cadastral instructions for licensed surveyors. New types of land records, for example, were introduced to reflect changing legal and planning needs. Yet these efforts largely focused on extending the existing system rather than rethinking it. Several attempts to modernize the cadastral data itself—beyond simply updating its surrounding applications—were made prior to 2023, but none fully succeeded.

A turning point came in 2023, when the organization’s CEO had a bold vision: to create a national digital twin with government and municipal data across Israel. This vision prompted the Mapping and GIS Division to revisit the cadastral system from the ground up, reexamining not only how the data is stored, but how it is maintained, consumed, and ultimately used in a modern, integrated digital ecosystem.

Defining Schema

Main Principles

  • Split the database into three “realms”: In-Process , Parcel Fabric and Management tables .
  • While the Parcel Fabric comes with a built-in schema we also considered what information is needed for:
    1. Other divisions in the organization (for instance “UpdatedByRecord” field)
    2. Private surveying companies
    3. Other government ministries
    4. Municipalities
    5. The public
  • Reducing the number of tables and domains to minimize complexity.

    Example:

    Both tax parcels (British parcels) and cadastral parcels reside in the same parcel-type feature class, differentiated by a Boolean field.

    The same approach is applied to the Blocks feature class.

  • Adding 3D cadastral (Multipatch) Feature Classes, along with their corresponding 2D projected (Polygon) Feature Classes into the Parcel Fabric schema. This allows for visualization on platforms that do not yet support 3D data.
  • Set fields properties (Nullables\length\Domains…).
  • Indexing necessary fields.
  • Designed a schema diagram (ERD) using DBML.

Applying Attribute Rules

Because Arcade expressions was new territory for us, we learned by doing a bit of everything: sometimes we'd write expressions from scratch, and other times we'd search for useful examples online.

(Only recently LLMs were able to generate valid Arcade expressions)

Validation Rules:

As for now, we implement 21 validation rules. Most of the rules focus on ensuring geometric integrity (self-intersecting or multipart features) and maintaining logical consistency within the database.

For example, several rules enforce uniqueness and sequential parcels numbers within their respective blocks.

Calculation Rules:

As for now, we implement 9 calculation rules. Mostly they include assigning parcels names, extracting coordinate values (X, Y, Z) from 3D-point geometries and retrieving GUIDs for related features.

Some rules ensure data completeness, for example retrieving the highest (latest) active parcel number of a block.

Next: ETL Pipeline & Integration

ETL Pipeline & Data Integration

Extract, Transform and Load

We designed a data pipeline to automate the migration. It starts by extracting all relevant tables from the old databases, which run on a “sealed” operating system, and transferring them to an open operating system under security supervision.

Once transferred, many data manipulation scripts handled the data transformation by cleaning values, fixing values, filling missing data and aligning the structure to match the new schema.

The resulting transformed data is stored in a File GDB.

And finally, we load the transformed data from that File GDB into an empty enterprise GDB, there, we can enable branch versioning, adding indexes and publish feature services.

Since the entire modernization project took nearly three years, we ran this whole pipeline every month or two. When the full modernization project completed, we ran the pipeline one final time.

Editing with Parcel Fabric

Preparing Editing Environment with ArcGIS Pro

We created a template APRX file with a map and a scene, which includes:

  • Default definition queries for the layers.
  • Symbology & labels
  • Pop-ups configurations
  • Scales dependencies (labels & layer visualization)
  • Hiding nonessential fields

This template APRX is used by automated workflows, serving as the starting point for cadastral editors when initiating their record-editing tasks (described below). It helps streamline work by preserving predefined GIS settings and reducing setup time in ArcGIS Pro.

Designing Editing Workflow with Tasks

In the Israeli cadaster there are about 15 different types of cadastral plans (or record types) so instead of creating a unique task for each one, we grouped them into 5 general tasks.

How we did it?

We treated the term “record” as a combination of inputs and outputs (like an equation).

For example:

  • Some plans keep the same input parcels but improve their boundaries to be more accurate. For these, we created the “Improve Current Cadaster” task.
  • Other plans will retire the input parcels and create new ones in their place. Hence, we created the “Retire & Create Cadaster” task.

These first two tasks are the most common ones that we will use. There’s also a version of the “Retire & Create Cadaster” task for 3D plans.

  • Moreover, some plans don’t have any input parcels and simply create new ones from scratch. These will be handled by the “Create New Cadaster” task.
  • Finally, we also added a “Free Edit” task for changes that aren’t tied to a specific cadaster plan - like fixing typos, correcting overlaps, closing gaps and so on...

Overall, all of the tasks (except the Free Edit task) follow a similar structure of steps but has differences in their tools. Most of the steps rely on Python scripting tools that combine Parcel Fabric tools or other ArcGIS geoprocessing tools. Here is a detailed overview of a typical task:

Step 1: Start Record Editing

The first step is fully automated and it prepares the data and the map for editing:

  • It begins with an early and quick validations to ensure the editor doesn’t start working with incorrect data. For example, verify that the user is signed-in to the Portal and that the cadastral plan status is approved by the reviewer.
  • It then creates a new branch version and transfers the Parcel Fabric layers to it.
  • Later, the map layers are filtered to the area of the plan by using definition queries.
  • The plan’s boundary is added to the Records layer and that new record is activated.
  • Finally, this step compute a report of any conflicts between the plan points (source points) and the active fabric points. The conflict report is intended to assist the editor in the next step by suggesting an optimal distance to use in the second step, helping to avoid conflicts. This action is optional.

Note: In "Retire And Create" task type this step will also automatic retire the input parcels and lines and add the new parcels and lines from the plan data.

Step 2: Import or Modify Points

In the second step, we use a costume version of the Import Parcel Fabric Points (IPFP) GP tool.

The IPFP tool is abuilt in Parcel Fabric tool that aligns the active points according to the points from the plan (aka source points) by moving them to the correct locations (like a magnet does).

And if it necessary, new points can be created to match the points from the plan. And as the points move, the related parcel, blocks and lines move accordingly.

We customized this tool to improve efficiency by implementing the following enhancements:

b. Automated parameter handling

Tool parameter values are filled automatically and hidden from the user interface. For example:

  • Instead of manually entering the record name, the tool retrieves the currently active record name using CIM.
  • The Proximity Matching method is set as a fixed (constant) option.
  • The command is restricted to run only on active points by applying a constant query.

b. Visual conflict representation

When conflicts are detected, the tool automatically generates red ring-shaped polygons on the map, rather than outputting the results to a database table. The polygon radius is defined by the tool’s search distance parameter, allowing editors to quickly and visually identify conflict locations.

c. Structured logging output

The tool’s log messages were customized to be displayed in a tabular format, making the results easier to read and interpret for editors.

Step 3: Update Attributes

Next, once all of the fabric features are aligned with all the plan features:

The tool here will automatically update the fabric layers attributes based on the plan data. For instance, the old land designation of a parcel is replaced with the newer land designation. This step will also run the Build Active command if the editor set this parameter on.

Step 4: Evaluation

Step number 4 provide a set of post-edit evaluation tests on the edited area. The editor can choose to run all tests or only specific ones. Additionally, the editor can choose whether the selected evaluations run on the entire Area of Interest (AOI), the record extent, or the current visible camera extent. This flexible option is especially useful for handling large, complex records or areas with a high density of errors.

The goal here is to ensure there are no errors within the edited area. The editor can run these evaluations as many times as needed before proceeding to the final step.

The evaluation step can be considered as a collection of (mostly built-in) tools that evaluate or track:

  • Validation attribute rules
  • Topology rules
  • Adjacent active points
  • Disconnected active points
  • Gaps and overlaps
  • Stated versus calculated area bias
  • Volumetric overlaps (for 3D parcels and their subtractions).

Step 5: Completion

Finally, once all the errors are cleared, step number 5 will:

  • Document the changes applied in the branch version (using the Version Manager service).
  • Perform reconcile & post to the edit version towards the default version.
  • Send feedback to the Cadaster Management System to confirm that the task is completed.

Summary

This task structure supports an almost fully automated workflow, where most of the hands-on editing occurs in the second step. In this step, active points need to be aligned with the source points. The time required to complete this step mainly depends on the editor’s experience, the number of points to update, and their density within the area.

Another time-consuming part of the process is the Evaluation step. The duration of this step depends on the editor’s experience, the number of errors found during editing, and any existing errors present before the current record editing.

Next: Challenges

Challenges

1. Performance Issues (Map Rendering)

The most critical issue we encountered was related to the rendering of features on the map while we were editing. It was very difficult for us to pinpoint what was causing the problem, and for a moment, we thought we had tried every performance tip and suggestion. Eventually, what saved us from the misery was reinstalling the SDE on the database server and recreating the database from scratch. The previous one likely had many issues and noises because we had performed many testing and made changes in the settings too many times. In addition, when we upgraded to ArcGIS Pro 3.5, we experienced even better and smoother performance.

2. Learning & Adopting New Technology

Since Parcel Fabric was new to us, it took us time to understand its logic, discover and experiment with the tools, and automate them. We later teach the editors how to use the tools and wrote a short user guide.

3. Editing 3D Data

Currently, 3D editing is limited in ArcGIS Pro and is not fully supported in the Parcel Fabric product. Hence we needed to find workarounds for adding or modifying 3D data.

4. Software Bugs

Along the way we have reported 9 bugs in ArcGIS Pro.

5. National Security

Completing projects during times of conflict is inherently challenging, and this challenge is significantly amplified when daily work is disrupted by ongoing rocket attacks, frequent air-raid alarms, and the constant need to seek shelter. Throughout this period, our systems were required to undergo numerous updates and operational restrictions to defend against heightened cyber threats, often while infrastructure and connectivity were unstable. In parallel, many team members were called up for reserve military duty, creating staffing gaps and loss of continuity, while additional workdays were lost due to direct security incidents. Despite working under continuous stress, uncertainty, and physical danger, the team remained committed to meeting project deadlines, demonstrating resilience and adaptability under extraordinary national security conditions.

Next: What's Next?

What's Next?

The following next points outline the strategic direction for the continued evolution of the modernization.

1. Relocate to Cloud

Relocate the database and supporting services to a secure government cloud environment (Project Nimbus, currently in beta). This move will improve scalability, resilience, availability, and compliance with government-level security standards, while laying the foundation for future cloud-native capabilities.

2. Improving Data Quality & Completeness Enhancement

Continue systematic improvement of the quality, accuracy, and completeness of existing datasets. This includes refining geometries, validating attributes, closing data gaps, and strengthening quality assurance processes to ensure reliable and authoritative cadastral information.

3. Tasks and Tool Optimization

Further optimize tasks, workflows, and supporting tools to improve performance, stability, and efficiency. Emphasis will be placed on reducing processing time, minimizing manual intervention, simplifying complex steps, and enhancing the overall user experience for editors.

4. Multilingual Support and Accessibility

Develop fully translated versions of tasks and interfaces in additional languages (Arabic and Russian). This will increase accessibility, reduce misunderstandings, and support a more diverse group of editors.

5. Comprehensive Documentation and Knowledge Sharing

Produce a more detailed and structured user guide and tutorials covering task workflows, tools, best practices, common pitfalls, and troubleshooting. The guide will serve both as onboarding material for new users and as a reference for experienced editors.

6. Advancing the 3D Cadaster

Progress the 3D cadaster framework by converting existing multipatch geometries into standardized Esri 3D Object formats. This step will improve interoperability, visualization, and long-term maintainability of 3D cadastral data.

7. Monitoring & Analytics

Establish systematic monitoring and analysis of task logs to support data-driven improvements. Key questions to be addressed include:

  • Which task steps are the most time-consuming or error-prone?
  • What is the average and variance of task completion times based on historical data?
  • Are there recurring bottlenecks associated with specific tools or workflow steps?
  • Do certain editors consistently encounter difficulties that indicate a need for training, workflow refinement, or tool enhancement?
